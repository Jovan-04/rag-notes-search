# Psychological Disorders

Other information needed before determining a disorder-

- Context (trauma history, other presenting concerns, situational events, etc)
- Demographic variables
- Duration/history of symptoms
- Intensity/severity of symptoms
- Family history

Psychological Abnormality-

   **Deviation**: behavior that is marked different from their society's ideas of proper functioning, atypical behavior, not culturally expected

   **Dysfunction**: symptoms that interfere with day-to-day functioning across different life domains, functional interference, a breakdown in either cognitive, emotional, or behavioral functioning

   **Distress**: feelings of pain, sadness, or unpleasantness to the person (not psychological disorders mean someone experiences distress Ex. mania, antisocial personality disorder)

   **Danger**: *not a common characteristic of those with psychological disorders* Behavior is consistently careless, hostile or confused, person is at risk for harm to self and/or others (more common for it to be harm towards self)

Psychological Disorder: behavioral, psychological, or biological dysfunctions that are unexpected in their cultural context and associated with present distress and impairment in functioning, or increased risk of suffering, death, pain, or impairment

DSM-5: Diagnostic and statistical manual of mental disorders, the most current iteration of manual for diagnosing mental disorders put out by the American Psychiatric Association, used mostly just in US

**Anxiety Disorders:**

   Fear or nervousness that is excessive, irrational, and maladaptive

      Anxiety is part of normal, adaptive fight-or-flight response (sympathetic nervous system activation)

   It becomes a disorder when there is a *unjustifiable degree, duration, or source of anxiety* and *disrupts normal daily functioning*

Acute Stress Response:

   Increased blood flow to brain due to increased production of catecholamines which helps facilitate cognitive performance

   Pupils dilate/peripheral vision is reduces (tunnel vision)

   Heart rate increases

   Faster and deeper breathing

   Increased muscle flow to larger muscle groups

   Adrenal hormones released, resulting in increased energy mobilization

   Digestion slows dramatically

   Blood pressure increases

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)- DSM 5 symptoms: Excessive worry and anxiety about a number of different events or activities

   Difficult to control the worry

   Physical symptoms (at least 3 in adults; 1 in children)

      restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance

   More days than not for at least 6 months

   Clinically significant distress of impairment

Specific Phobia- DSM 5 symptoms: Persistent and *irrational* fears of particular objects, activities, or situations

   Marked fear or anxiety about a specific object or situation

   Exposure to feared object results in intense fear or anxiety

   The fear is out of proportion to the actual danger

   Avoidance of feared object/situation or endured with intense fear or anxiety

   Significant distress or impairment

   Lasts at least 6 months

Social Anxiety Disorder- DSM 5 symptoms: Severe, persistent, and irrational fears of social or performance situations in which embarrassment may occur

   Marked fear or anxiety about one or more social situations

      - in children, must occur in peer settings

   Fear that will act in a way, or show anxiety symptoms, that will negatively evaluated

   The social situations almost always provoke fear or anxiety

   Avoidance of feared situations

   Fear or anxiety is out of proportion to the situation

   Lasts for 6 months or more

   Significant distress or impairment

Panic Disorder: Unexpected, severe panic attacks

   Short (<10 min) and intense

   Rush of physical symptoms and frightening thoughts (autonomic arousal like heart racing, chest pains, smothering sensation, dizzy)

   Thoughts of dying or going crazy

   Vicious cycle

   Can limit daily activities

**Obsessive Compulsive Disorder (OCD)**: Intrusive thoughts and behaviors

   Obsessions: unwanted, inappropriate and persistent thoughts

   Compulsions: repetitive, stereotyped behaviors that reduce stress or prevent some dreaded event from happening

**Mood Disorders**: relatively long, stable affective state characterized by series of characteristic emotions, can be positive or negative

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD): Presence of major depressive episode (MDE) without history of manic or hypomanic episode. Clinically significant impairment of distress

   Five of the following symptoms during same 2-week period (at least one of which is either persistently depressed mood of anhedonia)

      - Persistently depressed mood
      - Significant loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia)
      - Significant weight or appetite changes
      - Sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia)
      - Psychomotor retardation or agitation
      - Fatigue or loss of energy
      - Feeling worthless or guilty
      - Concentration or decision making difficulties
      - Recurrent thoughts of death, suicidal ideation, or attempts

Bipolar Disorders- DSM 5:

Manic Episode (DSM 5): Period of abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood and persistently increased goal-directed activity or energy, lasting at least 1 week

   Must include 3 or more of the following:

      - inflated self-esteem/grandiosity
      - decreased need for sleep
      - more talkative and pressure to keep talking
      - flight of ideas/racing thoughts
      - distractibility
      - increased in goal-directed activity or psychomotor agitation
      - excessive involvement in pleasurable activities that have potential for painful consequences

   Marked impairment and/or hospitalization

Hypomania: marked by just four days, still impairment to functioning but not to the point of hospitalization (same marked characteristics as mania)

Bipolar I Disorder (DSM 5): Having 1 or more manic episodes

   *Typically includes 1 or more major depressive episodes as well*

Bipolar II Disorder (DSM 5): Occurrence of 1 or more major depressive episodes and at least 1 hypomanic episode **no manic episodes**

Post-traumatic Stress Disorder- DSM 5: Exposure to actual or thretened death, serious injury, or sexual violence in one (or more) of the following ways

   - direct experience
   - witnessing the event
   - learning that the event occurred to a close family member or friend
   - experiencing repeated or extreme exposure to aversive details of the event

   AND must include symptoms in the following categories

      - Intrusion symptoms (flashbacks, distressing dreams/memories, etc)
      - Avoidance symptoms (avoiding internal reminders of an event and/or avoid any situation which could bring up distressing feelings)
      - Cognitive and mood symptoms (anger, sadness, etc)
      - Arousal and reactivity symptoms (increased irritability, increased hyper vigilance, increased or exaggerated startle response, etc)

   Possible triggers for acute and PTSD stress disorders:

   - combat
   - disaster and accidents
   - victimization
      - sexual assault and rape
   - terrorism
   - torture

Schizophrenia Spectrum and other psychotic disorders

Psychosis: state defined by a loss of contact with reality

   Hallucination and/or delusions

Schizophrenia- DSM 5: psychotic disorder in which personal, social, and occupational functioning deteriorate as a result of strange perceptions, unusual emotions, and motor abnormalities (affects all aspects of functioning)

   Must present at least two of the following symptoms, each present for a significant portion of time during a one-month period:

      - delusions
      - hallucinations
      - disorganized speech
      - grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
      - negative symptoms

   Functioning markedly below the level achieved prior to onset

   Continuous signs of the disturbance for at least six months, at least one month of which includes symptoms in full active form (as opposed to attenuated form)

   Three categories of symptoms:

      - positive
      - negative
      - disorganized

   Positive Symptoms- bizarre additions to a person's behavior

      Delusions:

         - of control
            - thought insertion
            - thought withdrawal
         - of persecutions
         - of reference
         - of grandeur
         - somatic delusions
         - nihilistic delusions
         - erotomaniac delusions

      Hallucinations:

         - auditory
         - visual
         - olfactory
         - tactile
         - somatic
         - gustatory

   Negative Symptoms- characteristics that are **lacking** in an individual

      - poverty of speech (alogia)
      - blunted and flat affect
      - loss of volition
      - anhedonia
      - social withdrawal

   Disorganized Symptoms- include a variety of erratic behaviors that affect speech, motor behavior, and emotional reactions

      Cognition/Speech

         - loose associations (derailment)
         - neologisms
         - perseverations
         - clang

      Affect

         - emotions do not fit situation
         - inappropriate shifts in mood
         - at times, may be result of other symptoms of the disorder

Therapy

   Directive: when the therapist is guiding the session towards a goal (more structured)

   Non-directive: when the therapist lets the client guide the session

   Open Ended: not constrained by time limit

   Time-limited: constrained by time limit

   Insight: approaches to therapy emphasizing improvement through a better understanding of one's own unconscious, dynamic conflicts

   Action: approaches to therapy look at direct changed behaviors irrespective of understanding why they occur

   Individual

   Group (including couples therapy, family, etc)

   Psychoanalysis (normally not used today) and psychodynamic (more common but not as much anymore)

   Cognitive-Behavioral *empirically studied and supported*- includes psycho-education, exposure, desensitization, relaxation, cognitive re-structuring, interpersonal therapy, mindfulness and are well supported in the literature across several diagnostic categories

      ACT- acceptance and commitment therapy

      **CBT- cognitive behavioral therapy**: idea that what we think affects how we feel/act, what we do affects how we think and feel, and what we feel affects how we think and act

         - Psychological problems are based, in part, on faulty or unhelpful ways of thinking.
         - Psychological problems are based, in part, on learned patterns of unhelpful behavior
         - People suffering from psychological problems can learn better ways of coping with them, thereby relieving their symptoms and becoming more effective in their lives

         Thinking: CBT helps people to learn to recognize one's distortion in thinking that are creating problems, and then reevaluate them in light of reality. Through that they gain a better understanding of the behavior and motivation of others, and through problem-solving skills learn to cope with difficult situations and develop a greater sense of confidence in one's own abilities.

         Challenging thoughts: What's the evidence?→ Am I confusing a thought with a fact?, Am I jumping to conclusions?. What are alternatives?, Am I assuming my view of things is the only one possible?

         What's the effect of thinking the way I do?→ What are advantages and disadvantages of thinking this way? Am I asking questions that have no answers?. What thinking errors am I making.

         Helping behaviors: don't be afraid of silence, ask open ended questions, clarify questions, avoid giving advice,, accept the person's frame of reference, maintain confidentiality

      DBT- dialectical behavioral therapy

      ERP- emotional response

   Humanistic 1: Client-Centered Therapy- individual being treated talks without direction, judgement, or interpretation from the therapist

      Unconditional Positive Regard

      Empathy

      Authenticity

      Reflection

   Humanistic 2: Existential- focuses on the ultimate concerns of existence (puts major emphasis on the choices we make and taking responsibility for them)

      Meaning- focusing on what is meaningful to us and how we can act out with these meanings

      Choice/Responsibility- taking responsibility for our actions and choices

      Death- realizing that we need to live in the present while also recognizing our limited time on this planet and what we should do with our time

      Isolation- idea that we are the only ones that actually know ourselves

   Humanistic 3: Gestalt- Emphasizes immediate experiences, expand personal awareness, and is very direct

   Integrative

   Eclectic

Antipsychotics (major tranquilizers)- reduce agitation, delusions, hallucinations, thought disorders through reducing effects of dopamine and lesser the effects on norepinephrine, serotonin, and acetylcholine (normally used for schizophrenia)

Anti-anxiety (anxiolytics or minor tranquilizers)- reduce anxiety, tension, and fear through enhancing effects of gamma-aminobutyric acid (normally used for panic disorders)

Antidepressants- counteracts depression, some antidepressants also counteract anxiety through enhancing effects of serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine, or any combination

Mood Stabilizers- counteracts bipolar disorder, reduces suicidal tendencies but the mode of action is currently unclear

Stimulants- counteracts ADHD and narcolepsy through enhancing norepinephrine and lessening effects on dopamine
