Action potential:

resting neurons are more negative on the inside compared to the outside, it normally rests at -70 millivolts (mV) which is known as the resting membrane potential. Or just resting potential. On the outside there are positive sodium ions chillin outside of the membrane. On the inside there are positive potassium ions but larger negatively charged proteins so overall the inside is more negatively charged. When a neuron is negatively charged like this it is polarized.

There are small poteins called sodium potassium pumps that are along the membrane of the neuron and all along the axon. These proteins take in positive potassium ions and release out sodium ions. For every two potassium ions it pumps in, it pumps out three sodium ions. This is why there are more positive sodium ions outside the neuron.

There are other protiens called an ion channel which allows safe passage across the membrane when their gates are open. Most are voltage-gated channels which open and close based on a certain membran potential. Sodium channels open at around -55 mV. Others ligand-gated channels which open when a specific type of neurotransmitter (ex. serotonine) attaches to it, and then there's also mechanically-gated channels which open due to the physical stretching of the membrane. When these gates open for whatever reason, ions diffuse quickly across the membrane and even out the electrochemical gradient, all while running away from the other positively charged ions.

An action potential is when an envrionmental stimulus occurs (spider brushing against your leg as an example) which causes the sodium ion channel to open (which is a voltage-gated channel). It needs to pass a certain threshold though (-55 mV) in order for the action potential to occur. This causes the neuron to become depolarized as so many positive sodium ions come in the neuron turns positive to 40 mV. This sends an electrical current down the axon which is lined by these ion channel proteins. As the electrical impulse moves down the axon each channel opens which causes the voltage around them to turn positive which triggers the next channel to open etc etc. Then repolarization happens which allows potassium ions to flow out which puts the neuron at a lower balance of -75 mV until the sodium potassium pump resumes its process to rebalance everything out and bring it back to its resting potential of -70 mV.

When part of an axon has some of its channels open (either in the depolarization stage or repolarization stage) it cannot react to any other stimulus. This is called the refractory period.

The stength of an action potential is always the same no matter the stimulus. However the frequency of the action potential Can change and does change based on the type of stimulus. The fastest neurons that are for exmple, in charge of reflexes, have their axons covered in myelin. This acts sort of as insulators which allow the action potential impulses to travel a lot faster than non-mylein covered ones.

Synapse transmission:

Electrical synapses are like text messages, super fast immediate modes of communication. They send an ion current which flows directly from the cytoplasm of one nerve cell to another through gap junctions (super small windows essentially). An action potential in one neruon will travel all the way down the synapse and cause an action potential in the other cells.

Chemical synapses use neuraltransmitters (chemical signals) and are much slower but more precise and much more frequent than the electrical synapse. These diffuse across the synaptic gap from one neuron to the next.

The neuron that is sending the message in a chemical synapse transmission is called the presynaptic neuron and the neuron recieving is called postsynaptic neuron.

The presynaptic neuron turns the electrical signal of an action potential into a chemical signal. This occurs when the action potential reaches all the way down to the axon terminals and activates another ion channel, the voltage-gated calcium channel. This then releases calcium into the neruon's cytoplasm. The flow of the positive calcium ion causes the synaptic vesticles to fuse with the cell membrane and purge or release their chemical messengers. The neuraltransmitters are then diffused across the synaptic gap and bind to receptor sites on the post synaptic neuron.

In order for the postsynaptic neuron to go into an action potential it needs to translate the chemical signal back into an electrical signal, this is done through the chemical messengers (neuraltransmitters).

There are two types of neuraltransmitters, excitatory and inhibitory. Excitatory neuraltransmitters make the neuron more positive causing the postsynaptic neuron to deploarize and bring it close to the action potential threshold of -55 mV. Inhibitory neurotransmitters push hyperpolarize the neuron which pushes it away from the threshold. This means the neuron won't go into an action potential to pass it along to the next neuron and means its less likely for that neuron to be excited by a stimulus due to its now more negative charge.

After this message is sent (which takes a fraction of a second) the neuraltransmitter kind of pop back out of the receptor site and are either lost to the void or taken back in by the presynaptic neuron in a process called reuptake.

Brain:

While a fetus the CNS starts as a weird tube thing. The tube's lower (caudal) end stretches out starting to form the spinal cord while the cranial (brain) end expands and devides into three pirmary brain vesicles known as the forebrain, midbrain and hindbrain. AS you continue to grow the brain parts continue to expand and form the five main chambers of the brain. The forebrain grows into two chambers, the midbrain remains undivided, and the hindbrain grows into antoher two chambers.

The lower most three chambers form into the cerebellum (coordinates muscular activity) and the brainstem, the midbrain which didn't divide into more parts is in control mostly of reflexes and te like, while the two parts of what were the forebrain continue to grow the most into the cerebrum (cerebral hemispheres).

Basically think of it this way, the hindbrain is in charge of keeping our basic bodily functions like breathing going, the midbrain is in charge of our instictual ancesteral stuff (the mid and hindbrain together are known as the reptillian brain because of this) and then the forebrain is in charge of all of the stuff that makes mamals and birds "higher thinking" animals.

We then have different sections of the brain called lobes. Our frontal lobe is in charge muscle management, motor skills, and cognitive functions.
