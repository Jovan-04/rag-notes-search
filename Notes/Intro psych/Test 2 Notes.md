# Conditioning and Learning

Learning- relatively enduring change in behavior or knowledge as a result of experience, two different types of learning

   Associative: classical conditioning and operant conditioning - how we compare certain type of stimulus

   Cognitive Models: latent learning and observational learning - more cognitive tasks learning

Classical Conditioning: neutral stimulus comes to elicit to a response because it is repeatedly paired with a behaviorally-relevant stimulus

When two events that occur at the same time the events become fused and soon the response to one becomes the same response to both.

Stimulus: an external event or cue that elicits certain types of responses

   Neutral Stimulus: an uninvolved stimulus that has nothing to do with a reaction/original stimulus

   Unconditioned stimulus: natural stimulus that causes an unconditioned response

   Conditioned stimulus: initially neutral but then associated with an unconditioned stimulus to created a conditioned stimulus which trigers the original unconditioned reponse from the unconditioned stimulus, which is called the conditioned response.

Response: learned or unlearned actions in response to a stimulus

   Reflexive/unconditioned response: unlearned, reflexive natural response to the unconditioned stimulus, naturally occuring

   Conditioned response: the learned response to conditioned stimulus

Acquisition: the initial phase of learning where the neutral stimulus is paired with the unconditioned stimulus to create a conditioned stimulus which triggers the conditioned response.

Extinction: when the conditioned stimulus is presented repeatedly without the unconditioned stimulus which will make the correlation grow weaker until the conditioned response decreases or disappears completely.

Spontaneous Recovery: when extinction occurs but then suddenly the conditioned and unconditioned stimulus start occuring together again

Generalization: tendency to respond to stimuli that are similar to a conditioned stimulus (responding to a buzzer when the conditioning stimulus was a bell)

Discrimination: the ability to learn to respond differently to various stimuli (responding differently to various types of bells like alarms, school bell, and timers)

Conditioned Taste Aversion (CTA)

   acquired dislike/disgust of a food or drink because it was paired with ailments

Operant Conditioning: learning in which behavior is determined by consequences

   Classical conditioning is the relationship between the stimulus and response, there is natural occurences between stimuli and responses.

   Operant conditioning is the relationship between the behavior and the consequence. The behavior that's voluntary and then the consequence that occurs.

Contingent: consequences depend on the action, these consequences can be reinforcing or punishing,

   Reinforcement results in increase of behavior

      Positive reinforcement means something is given/added, which increases the behavior in future
Negative reinforcement means something is reduced/removed/avoided, which increases the behavior in the future.

   Punishment results in decrease in behavior

      Positive punishment means something is being presented/given, which reduces behavior in future
Negative punishment means something is being removed, which reduces behavior in future.

Two types of reinforcers-

   Primary: meeting an unlearned biological need (food, water, sex, sleep)

   Secondary: learned vlaue (money, praise)

Shaping: reinforcing closer and closer approximations of a desired response, getting closer to the actual response you want. Was established by BF Skinner

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA): is behavior modification through close close observation, prompting, and reinforcement

Schedules of Reinforcement-

   Continuous reinforcement where every single response is the same all the time

   Partial/imtermittent reinforcement where only some of the responses are the same some of the time, based on time (interval) and number of responses (ratio)

Ratio- tends to generate more responses as we have direct control over our behavior

   Fixed: where reinforcement occurs after a specific number of behaviors

   Variable: reinformcent occurs after a different number of behaviors (and this varies around an average) [can be addictive like in gambling as theres always the feeling that theres a chance reinforcement can occur]

Interval- less responses as we have less control over time

   Fixed: when reinforcement occurs aftera fized time has elapsed

   Variable: reinforcement occurs after different amounts of time pass (which can vary aroundan average)

Superstitious behavior: occur because of random reinforcement when we are not clear about what caused the specific outcome as we then think we have influence over the results

   Ex. Skinner's pigeon (1948) experiment of seeing pigeons just doing anything they were doing before the food

# Memory

Memory is important for storage and retrieval of information which allows us to learn and helps us survive and helped our species to survive in an evolutionary standpoint

**Atkinson-Shiffrin Model**

The idea that there are three memory stores:

Sensory Memory- the brief but long enough to determine what we pay attentio to,

   Iconic memory: visual, .5-1 seconds long

   Echoic memory: auditory, under 5 seconds long

      "Cocktail party effect": when you hear name mentioned across a noisy room you can still hear your name being said

Short-term (working) Memory- limited duration of up to 30 seconds and holds info that we are *currently* paying attention to and processing about

   Is easy to "forget" and lose the information as we need to continue thinking about the exact thing over and over (repetition) but it also only really last ~30 seconds

   Chunking (which is combining info into meaningful groups) can help us remember things (think phone numbers)

Long-term Memory- lasting storage of information (think facts, experiences, skills, etc), the duration can be from minutes to years and is based on how often we use the information, the context of the information, the amount we know about said information, and just based on the person.

   Declarative Memory: memorizing facts and data from our lives, memories we can describe and verbalize and our explicit memories (think episodic/events and semantic/facts)

   Non-Declarative Memory: our implicit memories where we can remember and perform without awareness and procedural memories where our bodies learn how to do things and know how to do something, normally more difficult to recall or describe.

Control Processes determines what info gets transferred between the memory stores

Hippocampus is only in charge of new declarative memories (so can't remember how things work or describe how to do something) but we would still be able to perform tasks directly at hand

Types of Amnesia:

Anterograde amnesia- inability to form NEW memories after brain damage (normally due to damage to hippocampus)

Retrograde amnesia- inability to retrieve memories for events BEFORE brain damage (normally after trauma to the brain and time sensitive most severe for recent events)

Primacy and Recency effect, the effect that we pay attention to the first and last parts of a group of information (pay attention to the middle)

Shallow vs Deep processing, deep processing is applying the knowledge and actively assigning meaning to it

Encoding specificity: retrieval is best when in the same context as the encoding (environment-dependent and state-dependent), it's context based.

   More likely to remember when we're in the same physical location of encoding information as well as in the same mental state (emotional state)

Forgetting:

We're more likely to forget earlier on in the learning process, the rate of forgetting slows over time. But the ***relearning*** process takes less time than initial learning

   Study tip: review the notes the same day that you take them

Encoding failure: failure to process information into our memory, doesn't get past sensory memory

Memory decay: loss of memory due to a combination of a passage of time and lack of use

We can also forget due to interference from other memories (especially when we learn information around the same time, think learning two languages at same time)

   Proactive interference- old information interferes with newer information

   Retroactive interference- new information interferes with older information

Distribute the practice, space material out between breaks, study and then go to sleep

Make information relevant and meaningful, tie info to your own life and meaning of words
