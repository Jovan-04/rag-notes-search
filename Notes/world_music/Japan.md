#### Kata
- an aesthetic principle translatable as "patterned form" 
- the ability to follow or replicate a form is more important than its content
	- individuality within the form is generally discouraged
- examples:
	- tea ceremony
	- karaoke
	- flower arranging
	- sushi
	- martial arts

#### Japanese dramatic music
- Kubuki
	- Primary form of Japanese musical theatre; dominated by dance, similar to Beijing Opera
- Noh
	- Masked classical drama, often based on Buddhist Principles and teachings
	- originally the exclusive art of the samurai
	- older than Kubuki
- Bunraku
	- puppet theatre, featuring 2/3 life size wooden puppets

#### Religious or ritual music
- Shomyo
	- buddhist responsorial chanting of sutras
	- various languages and texts (sanskrit, chinese, japanese) depending on practice
- Kagura
	- shinto practice; generally consists of praise songs (torimono)
	- may be divided by court (mikagura) and folk (satokagura) practice

#### Japanese court music
Gagaku (ensemble of percussion, strings, and winds)
- Japanese court music (art music) has been under continuous imperial patronage for over 1000 years (ca 7th/8th centure CE) 
- Three major subgroups:
	- Togaku (Chinese and Indian influence)
	- Komagaku (Korean and Manchurian influence)
	- Bugaku (gagaku used to accompany dance)
- Wider array of instruments than hogaku
- Emphasis is on smoothness of line, serenity of mood, precision of execution, and lack of vrituosity
- Features jo (netori)-ha-kyu but for gagaku jo is the neotri
- Instruments have prescribed roles 
	- melody: hichiriki and ryuteki
	- abstracted melody: biwa (similar to the pipa) and gaku-so
	- harmony: sho (mouth organ similar to the cheng)
	- rhythmic punctuation: kakko (extrememly high tension larger "talking drum" type drum) and other drums

#### Hogaku
- generally catch-all term for Japanese traditional music
	- origined in the Edo period (1603-1867)
Sokyoku (music for koto, 13 string zither, similar to Chinese cheng)
Jiuta (trio (sankyoku) of koto, shamisen and shakuhachi, may also have a vocalist but not common)
Shamisen songs (shamisen and voice)
**Types of Sokuoku**
Kumiuta: collection of individual songs (uta--individual basic character for song) with unrelated texts for koto and voice (song set)
Danmono: sectional pieces (similar to rondo-variation form) for solo koto
	- each subsection is called a dan
	- may be arranged for sankyoku (trio featuring koto, shmaisen, and shakuhachi)
	- may feature a three part form called jo-ha-kyu (introduction-exposition-denouement)
		- starts slowly, but steadily increases in tempo throughout
Jiuta: features 3-part form (song-instrumental interlude-song) and sankyoku instrumentation
#### Instruments
Shakuhachi 
	- 5 hole end blown flute
	- may be used in hogaku or noh theatre
	- closely associated with samurai and ronin
Shamisen:
	- Three-string lute with a long neck; played using a large plectrum (pick)
	- derived from the Chinese sanxian; Okinawan equivalent is the sanshin
Hichiriki
	- short double-reed instrument
	- made of bamboo
	- 9 holes
Ryuteki
	- transverse bamboo flute with 7 holes

#### Kabuki
- First performed (according to popular legend) by Okoni of Isumo in 1596
- Flourished during the Edo period 
	- popular among middle-class audiences
	- stories either historical or folk-based
	- elaborate makeup, costumes, props, scenery, and staging
**Kabuki Music**
Two general instrumental groups
	Geza(off stage performers):
		- create sound effects
		- set a mood
		- voice characters' unspoken thoughts through song
	Degatari (on-stage performers)
		- provide direct narractive commentary
		- dance accompaniment
		- typically sit at back of stage on raised platform
		Two primary subgroups:
			-Chobo: shamisen and voice
				- deribed from bunraku; provides commentary on the story
			- Debayashi: larger ensemble, dominated by shamisens
				- tunes are called "nagauta" (long song); most are in jo-ha-kyu form
				- performers both play their instruments and sing
				- secondary, independent melody (taiko and nohkan or fue)

#### Noh Theatre
- Develops during the Muromachi period (1333-1615)
- relatively restrained and simple genre associated with smaurai
	deals with Buddhist themes of self restraint and self-discipline
- combines elements of folk dance, religious drama, and court entertainment
- Major noh figures:
	- Kannami Kiyotsugo (1333-1384)
		- established Buddhist context
	- Zeami Motokiyo (1363-1444) 
		- transformed noh into a refined court art

- Five Major Types of Dramatic Plays:
	God, warrior, femail-wig, possession/lunatic, demon
- Masks (nomen) used to identify the spiritual state of the character
	- shite (shee-tay): lead male chracters
	- waki: supporting actor, sidekick to the shite, usually either explains the plot or is there to have the plot explained to them
- Comic plays: kyogen
	- typically interspersed between serious plays
- Two-act plays typical 
	- second act addresses the spiritual transformation of the main character (shite) from experiences gained during the first act
**Noh Music**
- general term for noh music: yokyoku
- two primary singing styles:
	- kotoba ("words"): highly stylized speech-song
	- fushi ("melody"): more typical aria styles
		- yowagin (soft styles, appropriate for lyrical or reflective scenes)
		- tsuyogin (strong style, appropriate for warlike scenes)
- two types of uta:
	- sageuta (short, slow, low)
	- ageuta (long, high register)
		- kotoba style of declamation dominates
	- may be solo or choral songs
	- accompanied by small chamger ensemble (hayashi)
		 nohkan (flute), ko-tsuzumi (played on the shoulder), o-tsuzumi (the high pitched "pop" sound), taiko (larger drum played with large wooden "dowels")
		 - set mood, often through drum patterns (kakegoe); accompanies songs and dan es; marks entrances and exits; provides "incidental music" (just general music serving a general purpose)
