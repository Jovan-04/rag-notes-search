## Latin America
All of South America and Central America

Mestizo
	general term referring to the overlap and exchange between the various ethinic/cultural groups within Latin America
	- normally referring to the blending of Iberian and Aboriginal traditions
	- can also refer more generally to people of mixed=race ancestry (including of African descent)
	- Mestizo culture reflects blending and hybridization, but varies in its manifestations

Mariachi
	Originated in late 19th century as string band (violins, vihuela, guitarron) in rural Jalisco state
	Post-revolution (1910) mariachi features trumpets, more guitars, establishes standard rep and *charro* costumes
	- Features many styles of music; individual songs are often sectional or organized as a medley of tunes
		- presence of sesquialtera (hemiola) is common
Norteño
	from Northern Mexico, played by *conjunto* bands
		distinctive sound comes from button accordion as lead, backed by 12 string guitar and other instruments
	- wide range of styles (waltzes, polkas, corridors, boleros) but it will stay in the style till the end of the song/segment then move to the next style
	- highly adaptable (country music, tejano, *nortec*) 
		- notable for *canciones rancheras*, working class songs of hard living


Indigenous Aboriginal peoples
	Classified into two major groups: 
		- Lowland (Amazonian rain forest)
		- Highland (Andean and Mesoamerican)
	Instruments typically aerophones or idiophones 
		- Teponaztli (a log that is hollowed out and then have a slit in it through the middle and around, basically a box drum)
		- Tarka
		- Sikuri (a pan pipe)
		- Wakrapuku (a conch shell and fuctions similar to a drumpet)
The Aymara
	Indigenous people of the highlands (Bolivia, Chile, Peru)
		Speak localized Quechua dialect
	- Primarily farmers and ranchers
	- Broadly egalitarian society (social divisions among genders remain)
	Musical Instruments
		- Dry Season (April-October)
			- Sikuri (panpipes)
			- Kenas (end-notched flutes)
		- Rainy Season (November-March)
			- Tarkas (recorder-like vertical flutes)
		- Instruments played year-round
			- Pitus (tranverse, paralel to the ground, flutes, and drums)
		- Aymara Music is almost entirely instrumental however instrument playing is limited to men
			women may not play instruments but can sing and dance
		- Composition and poerformance are both cooperative ventures
		- Individual performances are practically non-existent, etiher solo or with group (soloist) 
	Performance is as much a social event as an aesthetic experience
The Suya
	Indigenous people of the Amazonian lowlands 
		- Semi-nomadic hunter-gather culture
		- Like the Aymara, musical focus is on group performance
	Predominantly vocal tradition
		- rattles or other light percussion optional
	Two different performance styles
		- Ngere(blending style, similar to Aymara)
		- Akira (complex polyphony)
			- very dense texture of individual lines
			- reflects place of individual within community
		- Is a stylistic segregation by age and gender
		- Like Amerindians, songs are "learned" from and represent aspects of nature 

Iberian Traditions of Spain and Portugal
	Languages and widespread Catholicism most apparent as lasting Iberian traditions
	Normally differentiated by social class
		- Peninsulares (Spanish-born and moved to New World)
		- Criollos (New Wolrd-born of European descent)
		- Mestizos (mixed-race)
	Concentrated in Argentina and Chile, but found throughout most Latin American urban centers
	Instruments usually chordaphones
		- Several variations of the guitar (Tiple, Cuatro, Guitarron, Vihuela, etc)
		- Bandoneon (a type of button accordian)
	Name of typical instrumentation (guitar, accordian) is 'orchestra tipica' or typical orchestra

African Traditions
	African musical influence is strongest where slave labor was most concetrated
		- Caribbean islands
		- Norhtern coast of South America (Venezuela, Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador)
	Instruments 
		Congas and bongos
		bombo
		marimbas
Afro-Caribbean/Afro-latin style traits
	- call and response and/or cylical forms
	- interlocking rhythmic patterns and ostinati
	- extensive percussion (esp. various drums)
	- very dense textures
	- Brazilian currulao displays many of these trains
		- main sonic difference is the use of marimbas
Candomble
	- Brazilian religious practice based upon Yoruban tradition
		- similar principles and practices found in Haitian *voudon* and Cuban *Santeria*
	- Adherents worship a pantheon of gods called *orishas* 
		- Aspects of a creator god (Olorun) representing various beings, objects, or concepts
		- Orishas may also have affiliations with Catholic saints
	- Ceremonies involve possession by orishas, enabling direct mediation with Olorun
		- Variation on Shona bira
	- Music varies by region or denomination 
		- drums, bells, rhythmic percussion dominate
		- call and response, relatively simple and repetitive rhythmic patterns, accompanied by singing and dancing among participants
Son Montuno
	- two part form originating in Cuba 
		- First part: either binary or strophic with refratin
		- Second part: the *montuno* featuring call and repsponse over a short obstinate attern
	- African influence displayed in extensive percussion
	- Important rhythmic obstinate are conspicuous
		- clave
		- cinquillo 
		- habanera/tango

The Tango 
	Emerged in 19 century Argentina; popularized in France in the early 20th century though
	- Quadruple meter, often syncopated
		- features the habañera rhythm, obvious or implied
	- Usually in binary form (sometimes ABA ternary)
	- typically features bandoneon (accordion), violins, piano, double bass, and possibly vocalist
	- if lyrics are present normally they're pessimistic or fatalistic; possibly erotic
	- medium tempo, appropriate for the complex steps of the dance 
	Compadrito: literally meaning "a man who has come to less", a dandy, charmer, slightly dangerous, outside of respectable. Slightly rouge-ish.
	Dance traits reflective of the compadrito
	- unbending upper body reflects self-confident stance
	- smooth patterns of movement mimic conduct of a knife fight
	- forward tilt of body dominates the female partner into a physical position of submission
		- may be enhanced by stacked-heel boots or shoes
