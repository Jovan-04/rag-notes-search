##### General Chinese History
- originally unified under the Qin dynasty (221 BCE); imperial rule lasts 2000 years
- long intellectual traditions, especially in philosophy (Confucianism--more of an ethical code/moral guidelines) and literature; Confucius (551-479 BCE)
	- similarly long-standing ties to the west
	- occident (west) -- in contrast to the "orient" (east)
	- extensive natural resources invite trade and foreign conflict (sometimes simultaneously)
- 1912-49 Republic of China founded by Sun Yat-Sen; still exists todays as Taiwan (kinda)
- Sun Yat-Sen was in charge of the Kuomintang (KMT) 
- 1949: People's Republic of China (the current "communist" government; PRC) established by Mao Zedong 
	- leads to isolation from west until President Nixon visits (1972)

##### Chinese Instruments
- Qin: seven string zither with no frets or bridges
	- 13 studs are embedded along the outer edge of the soundboard to mark finger positions
- Cheng: similar to the qing, but larger (16 strings) with movable bridges
- Yue Qin: the "moon lute"; a fourstring plucked lute with a round body
- Pipa: four-string, pear-shaped lute; short, bent neck and 24 frets
- Erhu: two string bowed fiddle with a hollow, cylindrical wooden sound box; one side is covered with snakeskin
- Sanxian: simiar design to the erhu, but has 3 strings, an oval soundbox and is plucked
- yangqin: literally meaning "foreign zither"; larger dulcimer played with bamboo sticks/mallets (like the Middle Eastern santour)
- dizi: transverse bamboo flute; additional hole covered by vibrating membrane
- souna: conical, double-reed oboe-like instrument
- sheng: "mouth organ" made of 17 reeded bamboo pipes on a circular base of copper, wood, or gourd, multiple tones may sound simultaneously
Chinese percussion:
	- huapengu ("flower pot drum")
	- gangu and xiaogu
	- wuyu (or muyu)
	- bang ban, zha ban, san qiao (varirous ban)
	- pongzhaong

##### Chinese Musical Practices
Traditional music relies relatively little on improvisation
based on memorization repetition, and idiomatic relaization of a limited body of works
	- may be organized by genre or instrument
	- parallels with western art music and philosophy (confucian thought on music's effects aligns with plato's)
		- suggests why western classical music and musicians have revently flourished in china

**Jiangnan Sizhu**
- literally meaning "silk and bamboo ensemble from south of the (Yangtze) river"
- performances today often done by amateurs
	- played by memory, usually learned through oral tradition
- small body of works (approx 24) centered around the "Eight Great Famous Pieces" (Ba Da Qu)
- typically consist of small groups (3-5 players)
- pieces tend to speed up
- will not include the qin as it comes from an already long lasting musical tradition

##### Qin Music
- the qin has a long association with intellectual and contemplative activities, giving it a refined status
- about 80 qin pieces exist; most have programmatic titles and sectional organization
- features two types of tablature
	- wenzipu: prose tablature; describes how to make the sounds
	- jianzipu: abbreviated character (i.e., graphic) tablature; more common notational format
	- dapu: the art of deciphering qin tablature
Form:
	- Sanqi ("Introduction"): slow with free rhythm, introduces the most important notes of the mode
	- Rudia ("Entering the music"): establishes meter and main motives, which are then varied by extension, reduction, or changes in tempo, timbre, or register; longest section
	- ruman ("Becoming slower"): main motives undergo further rhythmic variation; may modulate
		- may be followed by Fugqi ("Restatement") in longer works, revisting materials from the rudia
	- Weisheng ("Tail sounds"): slower tempo, played using harmonics

##### Chinese Music Education and Music for the Masses
- in early 20th century, pieces used in Chinese musical education featured certain standard traits:
	- short and diatonic
	- simple melodies
	- marchlike rhythms 
	- didactic (educational) texts
- Chinese music reformers like Fei Shi found this morally healthy, unlike court music (yayue) or theatrical music
- after the Japanese occupation of Shandong (Chingdao) province (1914), the didactic school songs were transformed into nationalistic protest songs
###### The May Fourth Movement (1919)
- establishment of academic music department at Beida (National Beijing University)
- Push begins for the collection and transcription of Chinese folk songs
	- traditionally looked down upon by the urban elite
- young intellectuals begin to criticize Confucian values, esp. social hierachies
	- leads some to marxism-leninism, promoted by diadactic folk songs with pro-socialist/comunist lyrics
	- under Mao, these become known as "songs for the Masses"
		- similar to practices already in place in the Soviet Union, guided by the artistic philosophy of "Socialist Realism"
##### Communist Reforms
- The Great Leap Forward (1958)
	- mass agrarian collectivization and attempts at large scale industrialization
- The Great Proleterian Cultural Revolution (1966-1969)
	- mobilization of Chinese youth (the Red Gaurds)
	- involves purging institutional bureaucracy; attempts to eliminate traces of so-called "bourgeois" cultural
		- ancient monuments, artworks, and traditions were destroyed or banned
- Contemporary musical ensembles
	- synthesis of Chinese and western practice (e.g. Chinese solo instruments and symphony; modern Chinese orchestra)

##### Peking (Beijing) Opera
- proper name: Jingju ("Theatre of the capital")
- Musical theatre genre combining drama, acrobatics, singing, stylized, gestrues, elaborate makeup and costumes, and dance
- dates to mid-19th century; stories often based on legends or historical events
 **Character types**
 - Sheng: lead male role
	 - Xiaosheng: young man
	 - Laosheng: old man
	 - Wusheng: warrior
- Dan: lead female role
	- Qingyi: virtuous woman (usually aristocracy)
	- Huadan: young coquette
	- Daomodan: warrior
	- Laodan: old waman
- Jing: painted face role -- male roles
	- intricate makeup indicates actor's character and personality
- Mo: minor old man role, less important than the Laosheng
- Chou: male comic role
	- exaggerated and highly stylized performance typical
**Jingju Orchestra**
Two major types of instrumental groups:
	Wenchang: pitched gongs and sizhu instruments
	Wuchang: unpitched percussion
		- trio of large gong (lauo), small gong (xiaoluo), and small cumbals (naoba) for big ensemble scenes
		- duo of naoba and ziaoluo for tragic scenes
		- solo for xiaoluo; tranquil or lyrical scenes
**Jingju Arias**
- typically based on 30 pre-existing melodic and rhythmic patterns (ban) 
	- sung in a stylized sprechstimme (declamitory style, midway between speaking and singing)
- four major aria types 
	- narrative: moderate tempo 4/4 meter, emotionally detached
	- animated (xipi): lively, with variable rhythm; used in scenes of heightened emotion, in a seven or five note scale
	- lyrical (erhuang): moderate to slow tempo; employed in tragic, lyrical, or contemplative scenes; often melismatic
	- dramatic: sung in free rhythm, but accompanied by ban and jinghu/erhu; enhances dialogue

##### Taiwan: The Republic of China
- Geographically and politically disputed locale for some 125 years
- refuge of the Kuomintang (KMT) and Chiang Kai-shek after the Communist Revolution in 1949
	- slowly transitioned to democratic rule by 1996
	- viewed as "rogues province" by the PRC today
**Wushi (lion dance)**
- Main tradition originated in souther CHina (Guangdong)
- Performed at important occasions such as lunar new year, local festivals, weddings, etc
	- often paired with displays of martial arts and zhangu ("war drumming")
	- may be performed by amateurs or professionals, men and women
- different lions and drumming types can convey different sorts of emotions and stories 
- notational format exists in a tablature format (pg 112 in EWM)
**The Puyuma**
- one of the sizteen officially recognized Yuanshumin (indigenous peoples of Taiwan)
- like other indigenous groups, their music engages with both traditional and contemporary practices
	- "campus folk song" tradition audible in Naluwan, but also use of specific vocables associated with Puyuma singing
