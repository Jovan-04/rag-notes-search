# Daily notes

***Day One***

**The Sachs-hornbostel system:**

- Aerophones
   - sound produced by a vibrating column of air
- Chordophones
   - sound made by vibrating strings stretched between fixed points
- Idiophones
   - sound produced by striking the instrument to create a vibration
- Membranophones
   - sound produced by the vibration of a membrane stretched over a resonator
- Electrophone *not from the sachs-hornbostel system as technology has not gotten to that point when system was created*
   - sound produced by generation of electronic oscillations (can be converted from a physical source)

***Day Two***

**Musical Textures** - all textures can mix and lead way to a different texture or have different texture elements

- Monophony
   - texture consisting of a single melodic line or part (does not mean just one player or octave, as long as it is the same melodic line)
- Homophony ("chordal style")
   - texture consisting of a prominent melody supported by a secondary, accompanying  harmony which is normally chordal
   - Homorhythm: homophonic subtype when all voices move in the same or nearly identical rhythm
- Polyphony ("contrapuntal style")
   - texture combining multiple independent lines, each of which largely retains its individuality within the context of the piece and are roughly equal importance throughout
      - can also refer broadly or colloquially to music with multiple parts
- Heterophony
   - texture featuring elaborate or slightly modified versions of the same melody sung and/or played simultaneously

***Day Three***

**Melody:** a succession of pitches, normally with distinctive rhythm and characteristic shape heard in the textural foreground (a tune)

   - the primary determinant of texture (but is Not indispensable)
   - can be laid out in a single part or constructed as a hocket (combination of multiple parts to create a single unbroken melody)
   - can move from voice to voice and enter or exit singularly or in combination to transform the piece's overall texture
   - descriptors: high or low? rising, falling, or static? narrow or wide? conjunct or disjunct? scale quality?

**Harmony:** pitches that sound simultaneously, typically supporting a melody and defining a key or mode

   - qualities of melodic pitch collections (scales) also implying the underlying harmony and vice versa
   - major and minor (diatonic) scales and harmonies are most common, but others are frequently found
      - 7 pitch modal scales
      - 5 pitch anhemitonic pentatonic (pentatonic)
      - symmetrical modes of limited transposition (whole tone and octatonic)
      - atonal (no centrally referential pitch)
   - Simultaneities referred to as chords
      - dyads, triads, sevenths, "tall" (anything "taller" than a seventh)
      - sonorities may be consonant (stable) or dissonant (unstable)

***Day Four***

**Time: Rhythm, Meter, and Tempo**

- Rhythm: organization of musical time measured in terms of the duration of pitches
- Meter: a means of organizing rhythm by grouping pulses (regular beats) into repetitive patterns
   - groupings made in ways that give greater rhythmic weight to some pulses over others
   - meter may be identifiable by repetitive ostinato patterns (esp. in bass)
- Tempo: relative speed of a piece as measured in beats per minute (bpm)

***Day Five***

**Time related terms:**

- Rubato: an elasticity of tempo allowing for slight increases or decreases in speed for expressive purposes
- Swing: sense of rhythmic lilt within a constant meter and tempo felt by the whole ensemble
- Syncopation: a temporary contradiction of the prevailing meter
- Hemiola: metrical effect that juxtaposes two pulses against three (an example of polymeter/polyrhythm
- Drone: long, sustained note(s) (normally within the bass line)

***Day Six***

**Ethnomusicology**

- a discipline uniting the concerns and methods of anthropology with the study of music
   - how music interacts within culture
- first scholar to propose use of the term was Jaap Kunst (1950)
   - used to be called "comparative musicology"
      - euphemism for music studies outside of the western classical tradition
- three primary subjects of interest:
   - music of non-literate peoples (when there is no written or musical record)
   - orally transmitted art music of Asia and the Middle East
   - folk music and culture (oral traditions found within regions dominated by literate practices)

**Alan Merriam's model**

- **Sound**: the music itself
- **Conception:** a society's basic ideas about music
- **Behavior**: the activities that go into making and consuming music

**Musical ethnography**

- **Ethnography**: the anthropological study of society, focusing on cultural origins and development
   - in music it involves identifying a musical scene and studying the soundscape to which it belongs (writing about the ways people make music)
   - how are sounds conceived, made, and appreciated
   - how these sounds influence listeners and performers
   - how are these sounds part of musical and social processes

***Day Seven***

**Other ethnographic issues**

- **Fieldwork**: research conducted while living among the people one is studying
   - may take place in urban or rural environments, developing or industrial world, west or east
- **Enculturation**: the lifelong process of "learning" one's own culture
   - social learning (socialization)
   - education (formal and informal)
   - schooling (special training or preparation for a specific task)

**Philosophy of ethnomusicology**

- musical world is very diverse and not everything fits into a western framework or view
- relativistic stances are often necessary
- comparisons may be quantitative but not effectively qualitative
- music is not a universal language but a universal cultural practice
- full understanding of music comes not just from sound, but understanding its cultural context and function
