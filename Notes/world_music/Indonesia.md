#### Gamelan
an ensemble of idiophones (normaly metallophones) 
##### Javanese Gamelan (or Javanese Court Gamelan):
Saron: one octave metallophone featuring metal slabs over a resonating chamber
	ranges include peking/panerus (high), barung (mid-range), and demung (low)
Slentem: similar to saron, but individual resonators hang beneath the keys
Gender (gen-dair): similar to slentem, but wider range and uses padded disk-shaped mallet to play 
Bonang ("Gong-Chimes") suspended in a wooden case on a web of cords 
	paired in 1.5 octave ranges of panerus (high) and barung (low)
Kenon: like bonang, but larger, lower, and each is mounted in an individual case
	Kethuk: kenong used to mark time and has a flat top
Gong ageng: largest gong, marks beginning of time cycle
	Other gons are the gong siymen or suwuk (medium) and kempul (small)
Non-metallophones:
	Rebab: two string spiked fiddle (bowed)
	Suling: end-blown flute (common in Bali)
	Gambang: two-octave xylophone
	Kendang: barrel-shaped double-headed drum
All pitched metallophones are laid out in two sets of scales (=laras)
- Laras Slendro (five-note scale, C-D-E-G-A)
- Laras Pelog (seven-note scale, E-F-G-Bb-B-C-D)
	- there are also patets, which can be think of modes which are only five notes. For a Slendro it determens which note to start on, with the Pelog it dictates which notes to get rid of
Ghending melody: general name for a gamelan piece
	- Balungan (literally meaning "skeleton"): main melody in a ghending, played across 3 octaves by sarons
	- Bunga (literally meaning "flowering"): subdivided version of the balungan, often played in bonang and gender
	- Gongan: single full statement of a colotomic (rhythmic) cycle
Gamelan pieces are organized in colotomic (cyclical) form. The Gong Ageng (gong) marks the *end* of the cycle
	In "Udan Mas": 
	- the kenong (nong) marks every four beats (1, 4, 8, 12, 16)
	- the kempul (pul) marks every four beats but offset by two of the kenong (2, 6, 10, 14) 
	- the kethuk (tuk) plays every two beats starting at one (1, 3, 5, 6, 9, 11, 13, 15)
##### Balinese Gamelan
- Balinese culture is more closely aligned with Hindu-Buddist culture; Javanese with islamic culture
- Dutch centralization of Indonesian government in 1908 (based in Jakarta) meant the end of other royal courts and traditions associated with them
**Types of Balinese Gamelans**
	- Gamelan gambuh
		- accompanies serious sung dramas 
		- there are related forms called gamelan arja but they are normally shorter and comic theatre as well as usually set in more modern times and are more popular though have a shorter history than the gambuh
		- quieter than Javanese gamelan; less percussion ("soft style")
	- Gamelan gong gede
		- literally meaning "gamelan with the big gongs"
		- resembles javanese court gamelan with the instrumentation and sound but only uses one patet(mode) of the laras pelog
	- Gamelan semar pegulingan
		- cross between gamelon gambuh and gamelan gong gede
		- features metallophones, but plays the music that is associated with the gamelan gambuh
	- Gender wayang
		- quartet of gender; may also have a small group of colotomic percussion
		- typically accompanies performance of wayang kulit
	- Gamelan gong kebyar
		- emerged after WWI; widespread by the 1920s
		- Kebyar means "sudden flare" (like a match)
		- an off shoot of gamelan gong gede 
		- resembles gong gede instrumentation but is much more virtuosic and unpredictable 
			- features kotekan, fast interlocking rhythms played on multiple intruments (like hocketing)
		- functional music, intended to accompany dance

##### Wayang Kulit (Indonesian shadow puppetry)
The Gamelan is often played during a wayang kulit
- most stories are taken from Hindu epics (Ramayana and Mahbharata)
- generally performed in poetic Javanese (similar to Shakespearen English) but there will also normally be comic characters that speak in vernacular that can act as interpreters and translators for the modern audience. An example is the clown character that is in many wayang kulits
- puppeteers (dhalangs) professionally trained and highly skilled,


##### "Authentic" Balinese Ceremonies
Kecak (monkey chant) and Barong-- a lion (with his friend Hanoman-- a monkey)
	- originally written by a German guy in the 1930s 
	- adapted to the legend of Hanoman gathering a monkey army to rescue Barong
	- not part of daily life, but more "authentic" in the sense that they are portraying their culture in a specific way for "outsiders" 
##### Indonesian Pop Music
Gambus--a type of lute but also a style of music
	- closely tied to Islamid (specifically Arabic) music practices
	- Instruments include gambus (left), dombek, qanun
	- texts are often on Islamic subjects (usually to make the music more "palitable" for a Muslim audience)
	- vocal styles and dress of performers may evoke Middle Eastern culture
Dangdut--fusion of rock, Indian film (Bollywood) music, and (sometimes) Islamic themes
	- name comes from characteristic drum sounds
	- dominant pop style in Indonesia
		- especially popular with middle-and working class young people 
Krongcong
	- contemporary style that has roots in the 16th century
	- originally combined Portuguese songs and instruments with Javanese musical practices
	- name comes from a ukulele-type rhythm instrument
	- also features flute, violin, cello, or Javanese equivalents 
	- instrumental acculturation was followed by formal acculturation in the 1920s 
		- use of blue notes, AABA forms
	- strong associations with post-WWII Indonesian life
Jaipong (or jaipongan)
	- relatively new style, created in 1974 by Ggum Gumbira
	- derived from the folk music of WEst Java (i.e. Sunda)
		- does not reflect foreign styles
	- ensemble called kethuk tilu
		- feaseures 3 kethuk rebab, gong, and drums; may include sarons
		- often accompanied by dance, either formal or informal
	- popularized across Indonesia as a "national music" thanks to TV and radio exposure
