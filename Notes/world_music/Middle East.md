##### Islam 
- third of the three Abrahamic religions, founded in 622 CE
	- its got (Allah) is the same one as the Christian and Jewish God; the Qur'an is its holy book
- its prophet, Mohammed, is the last of the great prophets from Adam to Noah to Abraham to Jesus 
- religiously egalitarian, though gender roles are often rigorously defined
- long-standing intellectual tradtions in science, literature, math, and art, including music
###### Five Pillars of Faith for Islam
Salat -- five daily prayers
Hajj -- pilgramege to Mecca
Sawm -- fasting during Ramadan
Shahada -- declaration of faith
Zakat -- charity, 2.5% of an individual's wealth

##### Music and the Qur'an
- No proscriptions against music in the Qur'an, but distinctions exist between halal (allowable) and harm (illegitimate)
	- catergories are not fixed and are subject to much debate and subjective interpretation 
	- best thought of as a spectrum of categories rather than a binary classification
- Musiqi in persian (or ughnia in arabic): music in the wetern sense
	- secular, composed, regular meter, instrumental, ensemble performance
- Khandan in persian (or telawa in arabic): music as "recitation"
	- sacred, improvised, free meter/rhythm, vocal, solo performance (ex qirā, chanting the Qur'an)
	- all liturgical works are khandan, but are not thought of as "musqi" in a western 

- Professional musicians typically exist outside cultural mainstream 
	- often jewish or christian, engaging both with ethnically distinctive music and religiously divergent traditions
	- dedication to art may rival or surpass dedication to religion
	- unorthodox modes of behavior
- Amateurs musicians are percieved as more independent/high-minded
	- free to choose "appropriate" repertory
	- perform for intellectual reflection rather than sensual pleasure

###### Musical Instruments
Oud ('ud)-
- arabic instrument with 12 strings, short neck, and pear-shaped body
	- forerunner of the European lute 
- traditionally played with eagle feather pick; plectrum common today
Other middle eastern lutes:
	- tar
	- dotar
	- setar
	- kamancheh
Zithers:
- Santour: trapezoidal 72 sgtring zither; played with two lightly padded balsawood mallets
- Quanun: slightly larger than santour; played in the lap with picks on fingers
Percussion:
- dombek: small, goblet shaped drum made of wood or clay
	- variants include zarb, tombek, darrabucca, darabuk, etc
	- played like a djembe, sounds like a tabla
- riqq: tambourine with two courses of jingles
	- often has a single head but some versions are open
Wind Instruments:
- Ney (nai, or nay): persian end-blown flute with two distinct timbral qualities (breathy and clear)
- zornah: double-reed instrument; similar to oboe but with flared bell
- zummarah: twinned pipes with parallel finger holes
Vocal quality: 
- lots of ornamentation
- uses some yodeling
- fairly tense, moderately nasal
- women tend to sing relatively low in their register
- men tend to sing relatively high in their register

##### Middle Eastern Music Theory
Melodic mode: maqām (arabic) or dastgah (persian)
	- 9 major (primary) maqāmat, 12 major (primary) dastgahs
	- like the raga, may carry extramusical associations (mood, place)
Rhythmic mode: īqā (arabic) or usul (turkish)
	- discrete rhythmic cycle defined by low and high drum strokes
	- hundreds exist, most between 2 and 16 beats
	- basic pattern almost always ornamented or embellished

Arabic, Levant (eastern Mediteranian), and N African performances feature suites in on maqām, each movement in a different īqā

Dastgah performances are largely improvised
	- based on short melodic formulae (gūshe) drawn from a larger collection (radīf)
	-  follows a general stylistic progression, but length varies widely
	- improivation may incolce ornamenting a phrase (layālī) or inventing a text (mawwāl)
	- quasi-yodelling style of tahrīr common in such performances

##### Aesthetics and Politics
Tarab: state of estatic musical enchantment
	- may be induced through either performing or listening
	- can ocur in either sacred or secular contexts
	- reflects broader concept of listening as deliverate action
Postcolonia politics have shaped Middle Eastern music after ca 1920, often along ethnic lines
	- non-arabic or non-islamic states, such as turkey and israel, still engage with aspects of arab culture in defining their own
	- ethnic minorities or marginal groups within states (Berbers, poor women, diasporic communities) may use music as a tool for protest or to assert identity
	- social media and western pop may merge with more distinctive local traditions (e.g. poetry) to promote political messages
