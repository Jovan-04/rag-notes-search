#### Candomble:
- percussion (drums and asuming the agogo) with symple rhythms that hold the beat and drive the song
- call and response, single male leading with community responding
- very fast tempo that slightly speeds up near the beginning and then seems to settle midway through and stays around the same tempo until the last 40-ish seconds where the drums (atabasques) speed up along with the agogo. 
- song is in Yoruban though people no longer speak it day to day it is used in these type of ceremonies and other religious contexts. 
- though this specific candomble song has clear sings of its African influence, there are other versions which have a much more "blended-cultureral" sound.

#### El Choclo
- dominated by the bandoneon, as common in tangos
- uses varrying dynamics to build tension and resolve, creating intense or pastionate moments as well as light sections
- though it may be because it is a bandoneon playing it, there is a lot of push and pull within the texture making it clear when the dancers would take long strides and also making the contrast clear when the dancers may be doing intracite footwork closer together
- it has a clear pulls and flexibility within the tempo with a "marcato style with firm accented and on-beat chords played on the bandoneon" (pg 333 EWM) 
- is in four sections (ABA'A'') with each section introducing a twist on the main melody.
