American culture constitutes an "imagined community" but many ethnic enclaves exist within it.
Individual ethnic groups more likely to assert a distinctive musical identity than tailor it to an imagined "American" ideal
	- Could be related to geography, age, religion, social class, gender, race, ethnic background, etc
	- various categories can overlap
most "mainstream" musical genres represent hybrid styles
	can still be employed in ways that articulate specific aspects of individual or group identity

#### Oral tradition
Music and/or text that is preserved or transmitted without tactile record (in non-written forms) 
Not exclusive to non-literate peoples or cultures
	recording technology invites the non-musically literate to engage in ways that would otherwise be off-limits
Functions both as a mode of preservation and transformation
	Two major divisions: traditions perceived as dynamic or static
		perception does not always reflect reality
Lining out- type of hymn singing when a leader leads the melody then others follow
Ballad(the Anglo-American Ballad): popular, narrative, strophic song, often short individual verses
	Sometimes called "broadsides" after the publishing format used after ca. 1500
		usually only text was typically published
	Often alternate between narration and dialogue
	May include a refrain
	Many doexens of variants (in tune or text) may exist for a given song
		variants: when one song can be connected very closely to the other whether in text or melody
	Brought over to the US by immigrants from the British Isles and Ireland
		frequently remained in use in the "New World" longer than in the UK
			particularly well-preserved in Appalachia
	Adapted in ways reflecting the US culture
Shape-note hymnody
	- name comes from shaped note-heads corresponding to solfege syllables
		also called *fasola* method
	- most common hymnal is *The Sacred Harp* which the first iteration was published in 1844
	Originated in New England and later flourished in the Deep South (especially in the 19th century due to the religious revivalist movement) 
	Overtly religious but non-liturgical, is a much more recreational activity and not sung during church service
	**Conducting**
	when the song is in two arm just goes straight up and down (down is one, up is two)
		sometimes when the song is very slow the person will do the pattern in four (similar to standard conduction)
	when song is in three the arm goes out first and then down then up (out is one, down is two, up is three)
	**Distinctive musical traits**
	- four parts, melody in tenor
	- Persistently loud dynamics
	- driving, regular 2 or 3-beat meters
	- parallel fifths and octaves are very common
	Major forms are: strophic, strohpic with refrain, and fuging tunes
		two parts: the first homophonic, the second imitative 
	Singers are organized by voice type in hollow square (tenor oppositie end of the pulpit/alter, trebels to the left, altos to the left of that, and then bass to the left of that) 
		leaders rotate throughout the session 
			other conventions: opening and closing prayers, singing the shapes, memorial lesson, dinner on the grounds 
	anyone may participate. for any reason
		"performances" are for participants, not the public -- is about building community
	Still is a living and active tradition, despite the archaic sound and practice
		growth of activity in the Northeast and Midwest since the 1960s
			conspicuously shaped by Southern singers
		there are multiple revisions/editions of hymnals 
			various oral practices present
		tunes are capable of taking on new meanings at odds with original intent 

#### African American Musics
- distinguished by longevity, wide dispersal, and association with the cultural mainstream
	- can be grotesquely distorted by outsiders (eg minstrelsy)
- Chicago jazz scene reflects early 20C scope and activity 
	- 65,000 African Americans move there between 1910-20, triggering the Jazz Age
	- establishment of south side "black and tans" brings in white patrons 
	- reaction against such clubs leads to the development of "sweet" jazz in white, north side clubs
	- Musical influences (gospel, blues, ragtime) intermingle
		- inspires apprentice system
	- Performance and presentation shaped to meet community expectations or conventions
**Blues**
	- normally AAB text form
	- 12 bar harmonic cycle (I-IV-IV-{IV}-I)
	- Blues Scale (C-Eb-F-F#-G-Bb) or just a pentatonic scale with a sharp four
	- Delta blues: acoustic, often slower, small, groups, simpler arrangements, rawer sound
	- Urban blues: more uptempo, larger band (often with horns), electrified instruments standard
		- reflects influence of R&B, soul, and other popular/commerical styles
