# Sub-Saharan Africa

**General traits within Sub-Saharan music**

- interlocking rhythms and melodies
   - hocket (splitting melody among multiple parts)
   - call and response
- aesthetic gravitation toward dense textures and buzzy timbres
   - focus on drums and idiophones
   - winds and strings are often used percussively
- cyclical, non-periodic forms prevail
   - usually involves multiple ostinati (melodic, rhythmic, or both)
- community or non-specialist participation is common (but not obligatory)
   - may include amateur musicians or dancers (those that don't get paid for their art)
   - musical events are often performed along with dance
- often features considerable rhythmic complexity
   - syncopation, hemiola, polymeter/rhythm
   - multiple rhythmic layers and subdivisions are common
   - interaction between a basic pattern and elaborately improvised parts

### **Sub-Saharan socio-political structures**

- Centralized kingdoms or states
   - complex, hierarchical bureaucracies
   - governed by hereditary rule
   - concentrated in West Africa
- Decentralized societies
   - relatively small populations; often clan-based affiliations
   - broadly egalitarian, typically lack formal nation-states
   - concentrated in Central Africa
- Republican or parliamentary democracies
   - typically post-colonial states (especially British and French); comparatively recent

## **Ituri**

Often called "Pygmies", however properly known by local clan names (BaAka, BaTwa, BaMbuti) as "Pygmies" is a more insulting name

They range across equatorial Africa, from Cameroon to Uganda; many in the Ituri Rain Forest; are a traditionally nomadic people and semi-autonomous

   - could enter into patron-client relationships with people in permanent settlements (entering into contracts for work for a short period in exchange for resources)

There is no formal leadership hierarchy or sense of "imagined community" (where you don't know everyone in the community but have a shared sense of belonging)

   - most important community is the clan or greater family
   - there is also a disinclination of private ownership

**Major traits of Ituri music**

- Group singing most prevalent style, especially for ritual ceremonies such as molimo (an instrument used in a ceremony to attempt to reawaken the forest)
- They do have small, light instrument that are easily portable (small drums, musical bows)
   - the mbela of the BaAka clan is an example of musical bows
- Hocketing is very common (combination of multiple parts to create a single unbroken melody)
- Rhythmic and melodic ostinati are common (often short)
- Call and response or soloist over ostinato are common
   - lead parts may change as the piece progress
- Normally heterophonic (focus on individual interpretations of a melody)
- Songs are normally made up of vocables or short passage of texts (which reflects divine incomprehensibility, simple to portray their thanks and love for their god)

## Mande

Located on the western side of Africa in the northern part of Sub-Saharan Africa

Have a clear organize class and occupational division

   Sula: unskilled workers

   - merchants, farmers, aristocracy

   Nyamalo: skilled laborers and artisans

   - writers, artists, musicians (jali or griots depends on the dialect), construction workers, etc
      - Griots/Jali are performers, historians, poets, social critics, diploments, and marketers.
         - Hereditary specialists
         - Ambiguous social status, respected for their power but live outside "polite" society
         - vocal music is central because of the elaborate treatment of the text
         - traditionally a male held position however over the past generation or so, women have been allowed to hold the position

**Major Points of Mande Music**

Most popular instruments is the Kora (also called a bow-harp or harp-lute)

- 21 strings
- they come in various sizes and are unique to the Mande
- there are variations on the design found in other cultures

Common formal elements of griot songs

      Kumbengo: short kora ostinato
Donkilo: main vocal melody

      Sataro: improvised or declamatory (more like speaking) singing style secondary

      Birimintingo: improvised instrumental interlude

**Major West African Instruments (majority used by the Ewe people)**

- Gankogui (double bells)
   - very important within the group
   - set basic beat pattern
   - normally used within percussion ensembles etc
- Axatse or shekere (gourd shaker)
   - gourd with a hole normally on top to turn the gourd into a more resonator thing
- Djembe
   - a "goblet" drum
- Talking drums
   - the Yoruban dundun, Ghanaian dagbamba, etc
   - have ropes connecting the heads and the body is more hour glass shaped
   - historically would be used to talk over a long distance and communicate (thus called talking drums)
- Mbira
   - plucked keys on a sound board (like a kalimba, thumb piano, etc)

## Shona

- dzavadzimu - the 22 key mbira of the Shona peope in modern day Zimbabwe, usually played inside a calabash gourd with bottle caps or other small objects for a "shaker" affect
   - can be accompanied by a hosha (gourad rattle, similar to a maraca)
   - has two complementary parts in mbira music
      - kushaura (lead)
      - kutsinhira (accompaniment) which can be rhythmically separated duplicate of the kushaura, or entirely independent
   - organized in cyclical ostinato patterns subject to gradual development

Bira Ceremony: Shona ceremony in which spirits of deceased relatives are consulted for advice or assistance

   - connected to animist religious practices of ancestor worship
   - involves spirit possession and community participation
   - Dance, music, and food employed to encourage possession (ceremonies idiosyncratic to a specific event or person)

## Afro-Pop

Many different forms, styles, and genres exist

   Often a product of acculturation (one musical tradition influencing the development of another)

Highlife: West African (esp Ghanain) style derived from European big band music of the 30s

   sung in vernacular; features indigenous percussion

Palm-wine/Juju: West African (esp. Nigerian) style combining guitar and vocal with extensive percussion
juju is amplified, palm-wine is not
