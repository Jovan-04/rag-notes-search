##### Main two systems:
Hindustani (north)
	- Mian Tansen (ca 1493-1586)
Carnatic (south)
	- Tyagaraja (1767-1847)
Many parallels with western art music
	- patronized by comparatively small, educated audience
	- extensively articulated bodies of theory and practice
		- performed in public concerts
		- may be entirely pre-composed, or may combine composed and improvised sections
		- virtuosity admired (within specific performative contexts)
Extended and influential diasporic community; sizeable minority groups in and around India
Musical theoretical system long predates that of the west
	- major split between hindustani and carnatic systems occurs in 12th century oral tradition dominates the transmission and prerservation of music
	- programs of study involve apprenticeship with a huru (in Hindu, pandit; if Muslim, ustad) for many years

##### Raga
![[Melakarta system 1.pdf]]
Organized pitch collections used in Indian music
	- analogous to scales, but carry extramusical affiliations (emotions, colors, seasons, deities, time of day, etc)
	- generated from the melakarta system, a collection of 72 seven-note scales
Melakarta raga: ascending and descending scales of the raag are the same as the parent scale
Janya (derived) raga: ascending and descending scales fo the raag differ from the parent scale
	varja raga: omits notes in ascent and/or descent
	vakra raga: changes direct ascending or descending
	bashanga raga: introduces a note foreign to the parent scale
 Other raga modifications:
 - gamaka: ornamentation (slides, turns, trills, etc) that reveal the performers true artistry
- stock phrases, themes, or motives indicating the raag's identity
- standard formal design for hindustani classical performace
	- alap: unmetered, imporivsatory melodic introduction that explores and establishes the raag
	- jor: section following the alap that introduces a pulse on the melody instrument
	- jhala: follows the jor; features fast, rhythmic patterns in the drone
	- tihai: trifold cadential pattern

##### Tala (pronounced "dala")
- refers to the repetiitive time cycle (meter) in a work, usually articulated by tabla
- often made up of irregular groupings or articultations of beats
	- first beat of a cylce: sam
- realizations of tala grow in complexity as a piece progresses
	- peshkar: series of variations on a give taal
- Hindustani music showcases this in gats (composed instrumental melody accompanied by tabla)
	- melody subject to variations (taan)
	- may alternate with imporvised passages (gat-tora)

##### Kriti
![[Scanned Document.pdf]]
- Alapanam: optional introductory section 
	- improvised, rhythmically free, established the raag
- Pallavi: equivalent to the A section
	- draws upon opening lines or phrases and may recur throughout the piece
	- may be estended by variations (sangati) after its first appearance
- Anupallavi: contrasting B section
	- may have its own sangati
	- may be followed by a breif, lively passage called kalpanaswaram (textless or solfege) or nirval (texted) before repeating the pallavi -- transitionary passage (think similar to a bridge) 
- Charanam: "verses" that alternate with the pallavi
	- may be followed by recap of anupallavi before final pallavii
- ragam-tanam-pallavi: extensive improvisation on a pallavi refrain fragment; not a formal kriti section
	- trikala imporivsation on the tala may also take place

##### Ghazal 
- Poetic style consisting of rhyming textual couplets (pairs of lines) and a refrain in Urdu set to music
	- sophisticated, elegant delivery
	- not excessively virtuosic, but uses gamaka 
- Mondern performances linked with jazz and other western styles; roots in Islamic and Hindu culture
	- poetic content may be secular or savred (the devotional na'at is an example of the latter)
##### Filmigit (or filmi)
- refers to songs from Bollywood film, not incidental music
- wide range of styles, many connected with western pop music
- frequently dubbed by "playback singers", not sung by the actual actors
	- Lata Mangeshkar credited with around 25,000 filmi recordings

##### Bhangra
- originially a folk dance from Punjab (north India) for men 
	- female equivalent was the giddha
- Most active scene found in diasporic communities, esp in UK and USA
- use of the double-headed dhol for deep, steady pulse and chimta for backing rhythm
- use of certain vocables (oy, hoy, and sometimes yo)
- strong quadruple meter, possibly syncopated
- often strophic, may have a chorus
- usually features a solo singer
- various substyles can be found that blend other pop styles (eg reggae-influenced bhangramuffin)
